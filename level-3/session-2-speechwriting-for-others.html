Phase 1: Context & Importance

Time Allocation: 15% of Session Time

The "Work-Ready" Objective

To architect high-impact communication for senior leadership, ensuring their message is precise, resonant, and aligned with strategic objectives, thereby amplifying organizational influence and your own value.

Commercial Relevance Script

Your ability to ghostwrite for executives is a direct multiplier of your strategic value. It demonstrates command of corporate messaging, empathy for the executive's voice, and the capacity to distil complex information into actionable, impactful communication. This skill positions you as an indispensable strategic partner, not merely a support function. In high-stakes environments, a poorly briefed executive risks credibility, market confidence, and strategic momentum. Mastering this means you directly mitigate those risks and elevate the entire leadership team's communication efficacy.

Phase 2: Baseline Calibration

Time Allocation: 15% of Session Time

The Scenario Setup

Your CEO, Ms. Evelyn Reed, needs to deliver a 3-minute address to all department heads next week, outlining the company's Q3 strategic priorities. She has provided you with a dense 15-page internal report and a vague instruction: 'Make it impactful and clear.' Your task is to draft a briefing note for her, summarizing the core message and key talking points she should articulate.

The Input Baseline (The Fallback)

A typical, insufficient briefing note would be a bulleted list of facts extracted directly from the report, lacking a clear narrative, direct calls to action, or consideration for the CEO's specific voice and the audience's immediate needs. It would read like meeting minutes rather than a strategic communication guide.

Phase 3: Technical Demonstration

Time Allocation: 10% of Session Time

Rule 1

The 'Executive Lens' Principle: Every point must be filtered through the executive's strategic priorities and the specific impact on their audience. Focus on 'What they need to know' and 'What they need to do,' not 'Everything there is to know.'

Rule 2

The 'Structure-First' Imperative: Implement a rigid, repeatable structure: Objective, Core Message, Three Key Pillars (with supporting evidence), Call to Action, and Anticipated Questions. This ensures clarity and prepares the executive for delivery and Q&A.

Coach Script (The Target Output)

Evelyn, for your Q3 priorities address, the objective is to align all department heads on our accelerated digital transformation and secure their commitment to cross-functional resource allocation. The core message is: 'Digital transformation is our immediate growth engine, demanding integrated execution across all departments.' We'll focus on three pillars: First, 'Enhanced Customer Experience through AI Integration,' highlighting the new CRM platform's rollout. Second, 'Operational Efficiency via Automation,' detailing cost savings from our new RPA initiatives. Third, 'Talent Upskilling and Retention,' emphasizing the mandatory digital literacy programs. Your clear call to action will be to establish cross-departmental working groups by end of month. Anticipate questions on budget implications and specific team resourcing.

Phase 4: Deliberate Practice & Peer Feedback Loops

Time Allocation: 60% of Session Time

The Global Speaker Bar Success Metrics — to pass, the student must flawlessly demonstrate all three tracking pillars simultaneously.

  1. Technical Accuracy: The briefing note's verbal structure must adhere precisely to: 'Objective (1 sentence) - Core Message (1 sentence) - Three Key Pillars (each 1 sentence, followed by 1 supporting detail) - Call to Action (1 sentence) - Anticipated Questions (2 examples).' Any deviation from this exact sequence or brevity results in failure.
  2. Linguistic Fluency / Bimodal Alignment: Physical Stage: Maintain direct eye contact with the 'Executive.' Use precise, deliberate gestures to emphasize each structural component. Project confidence and calm authority. Virtual Frame: Establish direct lens contact consistently. Ensure a stable, centered frame. Use vocal resonance to convey gravitas and clarity, avoiding vocal fry or uptalk. The briefing document itself, if shared digitally, must be visually sparse and impactful.
  3. Executive Presence: Zero filler words (um, ah, like). Maintain a steady, controlled vocal tone throughout. Exhibit confident posture (standing or seated). Demonstrate anticipation by briefly pausing before articulating each new structural element. Convey absolute mastery of the content, projecting that the executive is fully prepared.

The Core Drill

The Briefing Note

The Rotational Execution Guidelines

Students will work in pairs. One student acts as the 'Ghostwriter,' the other as the 'Executive.' The Ghostwriter receives a new, complex scenario (e.g., announcing a merger, a major product recall, a new sustainability initiative) and has 5 minutes to draft a briefing note using the prescribed structure. They then 'brief' the Executive verbally in 90 seconds. The Executive provides immediate feedback based on clarity, impact, and readiness for delivery. Roles then reverse. Each round is timed and rigorously scored against the three pillars.

Phase 5: The Complexity Shift (Conditional Trigger)

Stress-Test 1 (Time Crunch)

The executive has just been called into an urgent board meeting and needs a 60-second verbal briefing on a critical market development for her address in 10 minutes. Condense your briefing note to its absolute essence: Objective, Core Message, and one critical action item. Deliver it in 45 seconds.

Stress-Test 2 (Adversarial)

The 'Executive' now challenges your briefing note directly: 'This doesn't capture the full nuance of the market shift, and I anticipate significant pushback from the regional VPs. Your briefing is too simplistic.' Respond by reiterating the strategic intent and demonstrating how the current structure addresses the *most critical* points for the *intended audience and outcome*, not every possible detail. Maintain composure and focus on the objective.
